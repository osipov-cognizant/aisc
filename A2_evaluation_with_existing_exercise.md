# A2 in light of the existing CMT onboarding exercise
### Evaluation of how the natural experiment changes the protocol

---

## Top-line assessment

The exercise you describe is materially better than the designed A2 protocol on most dimensions, materially worse on a few, and changes the construct being measured in one important way. The headline points are these.

The 50/50 split between LLM-generated and workshop-generated transformation proposals constitutes a real randomized experimental manipulation that A2 had to construct synthetically. The peer-review selection of "most authentic-looking" LLM proposals means the LLM arm is composed of the strongest available slop, which is a more conservative test than A2's design (where producers were stratified by AI-usage intensity rather than by output authenticity rating). The blind interview structure — teams interrogating authors without knowing whether the proposal was LLM-generated — is the gold standard of blinding and is genuinely hard to achieve in any constructed research design. The 1.0–5.0 implementation plan grading by a 3-person executive panel is a multi-rater operational instrument that already exists, eliminating both the dependent-variable construction and the QA scorer training that A2 required.

The construct shift is that A2 was framed as a test of producer competence (can the original author teach a trainee to produce comparable work?). The CMT onboarding exercise is a test of slop survival under downstream use (does the artifact survive being passed through a competent but uninformed downstream team, who must extract value from it via interview and produce a graded deliverable?). The second construct is closer to what Osipov's overall thesis actually claims and closer to what the Niederhoffer et al. (2025) workslop study measured at the population level. It is also the more operationally relevant question for a BPO/SI company: leadership cares less about whether an individual associate can teach than whether the work product survives downstream consumption. So the construct shift is in the right direction.

The protocol can absolutely be improved by adopting this setup as the experimental vehicle. The remainder of this document specifies how.

## Section 1 — What this setup gives you for free

Several A2 design components become unnecessary or trivially satisfied.

**Brief library construction (A2 §5) is largely solved.** The 30-validated-briefs-per-line-of-business problem that I called the protocol's hardest design problem mostly evaporates. The transformation proposals themselves are the briefs. The LLM-curriculum-adversarial validation that I specified for brief construction has already been performed in a stronger form: the LLM-generated proposals were not only generated by frontier LLMs but were peer-reviewed for "authenticity" and only the highest-scoring were retained. This is a sharper adversarial filter than the one I proposed, because it incorporates a human authenticity judgment in addition to LLM-curriculum resistance. What remains for the research team to do on briefs is much smaller: ensure the LLM and workshop arms are matched on superficial features (length, complexity, client size, vertical sub-segment within CMT) so that the source of variance in downstream grade is not driven by, say, LLM proposals being systematically longer or addressing simpler problems.

**Trainee recruitment and matching (A2 §6) is solved.** The onboarding teams are the trainees. They are already in the BPO's pipeline, already participating in this exercise as part of normal operations, and they are not being asked to participate in research outside their job function. The matching variables I specified (education, prior experience, English proficiency) can still be used as covariates in the analysis but no separate recruitment is needed.

**Producer notification (A2 §0) is solved.** Authors of the proposals are not being notified that they are part of a research study. They are simply being interviewed by onboarding teams as part of an established process. This eliminates the rehearsal-anticipation problem that A2 §0 was designed to control.

**Pilot phase (A2 §11) is largely solved.** The exercise has presumably been run before. Historical data on grade distributions, panel inter-rater reliability, team composition variance, and time-to-completion already exist. What remains is a 1–2 week pilot specifically to validate the additional instrumentation (interview recording, screening-step capture, source revelation timing), not the full four-week pilot the A2 protocol called for.

**Hawthorne effects on producer performance (A2 §13) collapse.** The authors are not aware they are participants in a study. Their behavior in interviews reflects their genuine response to being asked to defend their work, not their performance under research observation.

**Labor relations and consent infrastructure (A2 §14, §18) becomes substantially simpler.** Recording of interviews and grading of implementation plans are existing operational practices. The marginal consent requirement is for research analysis of these existing recordings, which is a smaller and less contentious request than the full A2 consent package.

The cumulative effect is that the per-test cost in A2 §12 (≈$422) collapses to something on the order of $50–80 per test, because almost everything that was billable is already happening and only the instrumentation and analysis are marginal. The full first-wave program cost (A2 §12: $90,000–$100,000) collapses to perhaps $25,000–$35,000, almost entirely in research staff time for analysis and coding rather than in operational costs. This is a 3–4× cost reduction on the core study.

## Section 2 — What this setup does that A2 could not

Three diagnostic signals exist in your setup that I did not design A2 to capture, and each is independently informative.

**The screening signal.** Before any interview happens, each onboarding team picks which proposal(s) to develop. The pick rate of LLM-generated proposals vs. workshop-generated proposals is a direct test of the "superficial competence" property that Kommers et al. (2026) identify as the first prototypical feature of AI slop. If teams pick LLM proposals at substantially higher rates than workshop proposals, the surface-polish-as-decoy hypothesis is confirmed at the screening stage. If teams pick at chance, it suggests teams can detect slop pre-interview. The screening choice is a measurement that A2 did not have access to and that maps directly onto the workslop literature's claim that slop's surface fluency is what makes it costly downstream.

**The selection-grade gap.** Compare the relationship between the team's screening confidence (how confident were they that the proposal would impress the panel?) and the eventual panel grade. If teams select LLM proposals because they look impressive but those proposals then receive lower grades, the gap between expected and actual grade is itself the measurement of how much value the slop's surface features destroyed downstream. This is a more direct measurement of the workslop "invisible tax" than anything in the Niederhoffer et al. survey, because it captures the gap at the individual decision rather than averaging across self-reported instances.

**The interview-as-interrogation signal.** This is structurally different from A2's teaching framing. In A2, the producer was trying to help a naive trainee succeed. In your setup, the team is trying to extract from the author what they need to build a good implementation plan. The author has no incentive to help the team beyond what their own reputation demands, and the team has motivation to probe — they will be graded on the result. This is a more adversarial information-extraction context than the cooperative teaching context A2 imagined, and it is closer to the conditions under which slop genuinely fails in real organizational use (a downstream consumer trying to do their job, not a teacher trying to help a student). The author behavior under this kind of interrogation — response latency, hedging, deflection, claims of "that's a good question for the technical team" — is more diagnostic than under A2's teaching framing.

These three signals taken together form a richer measurement model than A2 supports: pre-interview screening, in-interview author behavior, and post-interview team output. Each of the three is independently scoreable and they give us a chain of evidence about where slop fails (does it pass screening? Does it fail the interview? Does it fail in implementation?) rather than a single discriminative outcome.

## Section 3 — What this setup makes worse

Three complications are real and need to be designed around.

**Two-step mediation noise.** A2 measured trainee QA score as a function of producer competence directly. Your setup measures panel grade as a function of (proposal type) × (team's ability to extract value from the interview) × (team's implementation-plan construction skill). The team variance is interposed between the producer signal and the dependent variable. This adds noise. With teams of, say, 3–5 people working on a single implementation plan, individual team-member variance is partially averaged within the team, but team-level variance — composition luck, group dynamics, leadership emergence — is not controlled for. This is the most serious threat the new setup introduces.

The mitigation is straightforward but requires deliberate design choices upfront. First, randomize team composition as much as the operational process permits (counterbalance team-skill levels across proposal types). Second, have multiple teams work on the same proposal where possible, so the proposal's effect can be estimated separately from the team's effect (this is structurally analogous to the random-effects model in A2 §9 but with proposal as one random effect and team as another). Third, include a baseline measurement of team capability before the exercise begins (a short structured task graded by the same panel rubric) so team capability can be entered as a covariate.

**Two-step transfer extends the LLM-curriculum exploit.** The teams developing the implementation plans presumably have access to LLM tools themselves. This means even if the original proposal was workshop-generated and the author cannot defend it, a sufficiently strong onboarding team using LLMs in the implementation phase may rescue a poor interview by post-hoc augmentation. The dependent variable (panel grade) is then partially a measure of the team's LLM-augmentation skill rather than purely a measure of the producer's slop-vs-not-slop status. This is a real construct-validity threat.

The mitigations here are partial and have to be chosen with operational realism. The cleanest fix — restricting LLM use in the implementation phase — is probably impractical because LLM tools are part of the BPO's normal work environment and restricting them would distort the exercise. The realistic mitigation is to instrument LLM usage during the implementation phase (via existing telemetry, browser monitoring, or self-report) and treat it as a covariate or moderator. If the panel-grade gap between LLM and workshop proposals shrinks for teams that used LLMs heavily during implementation, this is itself a finding (slop can be patched downstream with more slop-generating tooling) that has direct operational implications.

**Selection bias from team screening choice.** If teams systematically prefer one proposal type at the screening stage — say, they pick LLM proposals 70% of the time because of surface polish — the LLM proposals are tested by a different (probably better-than-average, since they were the picked ones) subset of teams than the workshop proposals are. The arms are not balanced on team capability post-screening even if they were balanced pre-screening. This biases the within-arm panel-grade comparison.

The cleanest mitigation is to randomize team-to-proposal assignment for at least a subset of teams, sacrificing the screening signal for those teams in order to obtain an unbiased estimate of the proposal-type effect on grade. A useful design is a hybrid: half the teams choose freely (which preserves the screening signal) and half are randomly assigned (which preserves the unbiased grade estimate). The screening signal is then estimated on the choice-half and the grade signal on the assigned-half, with the two halves cross-checked for consistency in the assigned-half results.

## Section 4 — Construct shift: what is actually being tested now

A2 was a test of one specific Osipov claim: that live supervised teaching of a naive trainee in a novel context discriminates competent producers from slop cannons. The CMT onboarding exercise is a test of a different and more general claim: that AI-generated work, even when peer-validated as authentic-looking, fails to survive downstream consumption by competent uninformed users.

The second claim is the broader Osipov thesis and is the one that aligns with the workslop literature. It is also a more useful claim for a BPO/SI company to validate, because BPO and SI work is fundamentally about producing artifacts that are consumed downstream — by clients, by other internal teams, by regulators. A test that measures slop survival in downstream consumption is directly informative for operational decisions in a way that a test of producer teaching-capacity, in isolation, is not.

The trade-off is that the new setup does not isolate the live-supervision mechanism specifically. In A2, the live-vs-asynchronous distinction was a primary dimension of the test design (synchrony was the LLM-curriculum-resistant property). In your setup, the live element (the interview) is bundled with the artifact-survival element (the proposal-to-implementation-plan transformation), and isolating the contribution of the live interview specifically requires additional design — for example, comparing teams that interview authors against teams that work only from the proposal document. If only one of these conditions is operationally available, the live-supervision-specific hypothesis (Osipov's H2b in the A2 deep-dive) cannot be tested. It is replaced by a broader and more useful test of slop's downstream survival.

There is a clean way to recover the live-supervision test within this setup if it is operationally feasible: add a third arm to the screening-and-development process in which teams develop implementation plans from the proposal document alone, without the interview step. The grade gap between the interview-condition and document-only teams, conditional on proposal type (LLM vs workshop), tells us how much value the live interview added — and whether it added more for LLM proposals (which slop cannons would presumably need to defend in person to give the team something to work with) or for workshop proposals (where the author's deeper understanding can be drawn out by interview but the proposal already stands on its own). This is a direct operational test of the synchrony hypothesis.

## Section 5 — Recommended modifications to the A2 protocol given this setup

Synthesizing the above, the following protocol modifications are warranted.

**Adopt the CMT onboarding exercise as the experimental vehicle in place of constructed sessions.** Drop the constructed brief library, the trainee recruitment, the synthetic producer-trainee pairing, and the controlled 90-minute session structure. Use the existing exercise as it stands operationally, with measurement instrumentation added.

**Add three measurement layers to the existing exercise.** First, instrument the screening step: each team records which proposals they considered, which they ranked highest, and brief reasons for their top pick. This becomes the screening signal. Second, record and code the author interviews (extending existing recording practices if necessary, with appropriate consent) for the author behavior dimensions specified in A2 §8: response latency, external-resource reference rate, direct-answer proportion, hedging/deflection markers. Third, capture the panel grade with each panelist's individual rating (not just the consensus) so inter-rater reliability and panelist-specific patterns can be analyzed.

**Stratify and balance team-to-proposal assignment.** Run the exercise with a deliberate split: roughly 60% of teams use the natural screening process (preserving the screening signal); roughly 40% are randomly assigned a proposal (eliminating selection bias for the grade-comparison estimate). This requires only a modest operational adjustment (the random-assignment teams skip the screening step or perform it as a recorded-but-non-binding exercise) and gives both estimators clean data.

**Add a document-only condition if operationally feasible.** A subset of teams (perhaps 20% of total) develop their implementation plan from the proposal document alone, without an author interview. This isolates the contribution of the live-interview step and provides the direct test of the synchrony hypothesis that the bundled exercise does not support on its own.

**Implement source revelation only after panel grading.** Teams should learn whether their assigned proposal was LLM-generated only after the panel has graded their implementation plan. This preserves blinding and also unlocks a post-hoc analytic step: ask teams to retrospectively guess the source, and measure whether their guess accuracy is above chance. If teams can correctly identify slop after working with it, this is itself a measurement of the gap between superficial competence and deeper inspectability.

**Add an author post-hoc survey.** After panel grading, surveying the proposal authors on (i) how confident they were the proposal would be selected, (ii) how prepared they felt for the interview, (iii) how they would have answered any questions they struggled with — is a low-cost measurement that captures the producer-side metacognition that A2 §7 specified as a secondary dependent variable.

**Track LLM tool usage during the implementation phase.** Whatever telemetry the BPO normally collects on tool usage should be retained for analysis. If teams using LLMs heavily during implementation produce different grade gaps between LLM-source and workshop-source proposals than teams using LLMs lightly, this is the operational measurement of slop-rescuing-slop, which is a critical finding for any organization deciding how to deploy AI tools.

## Section 6 — Revised primary analyses

The primary analytic model becomes a mixed-effects regression of panel grade on proposal source (LLM vs workshop), with random effects for team, for proposal, for panel composition, and for vertical sub-segment within CMT. The peer-review authenticity score for the LLM proposals enters as a covariate, allowing estimation of whether higher-authenticity-rated slop survives better downstream than lower-authenticity-rated slop (this would either support or weaken the Kommers et al. "superficial competence" thesis depending on the sign).

Pre-registered secondary analyses should include: (i) the screening-pick-rate model — does proposal source predict screening selection probability, controlling for proposal length and superficial complexity?; (ii) the interview-behavior model — do author behavior codes (latency, deflection, direct-answer rate) predict downstream panel grade, and is this mediation different by proposal source?; (iii) the document-only-comparison model — does the live-interview step add more grade points for LLM proposals than for workshop proposals?; (iv) the LLM-augmentation model — does heavy team-side LLM use during the implementation phase compress or widen the LLM-vs-workshop grade gap?; (v) the team-source-detection accuracy — can teams identify proposal source above chance after working with it, and is detection accuracy correlated with grade outcome?

The pre-registered prediction from Osipov's theory, applied to this setup, is now sharper than what A2 §9 specified: panel grade should be substantially lower for LLM-source proposals (hypothesized effect size in the d = 0.5–0.8 range, pooled across all teams), the effect should be even larger when team-side LLM use during implementation is held low (because heavy team-side LLM use will partially rescue weak source proposals), and the screening-pick rate for LLM proposals should be at or above chance — the hypothesis predicts that LLM proposals look impressive enough to pass screening but fail in downstream use, which is the precise operationalization of the workslop "superficial competence" failure mode.

## Section 7 — New threats specific to this setup

Several risks are specific to using the existing exercise rather than a constructed protocol and need to be flagged explicitly.

**Author-author variance.** The workshop proposals were created by associates working in person, presumably with varying levels of skill. The LLM proposals were generated by associates with varying prompting skill, then peer-filtered. The two arms may differ in average author skill in ways that correlate with downstream grade but are not the construct we want to measure. This is partially controlled by the random allocation of associates to LLM-vs-workshop conditions (assuming this allocation was random), but if associates self-selected which exercise to participate in, there is a confound. The first analytic step should be checking how the LLM-vs-workshop arm assignment was made and whether selection effects exist.

**Peer-review authenticity-score bias.** The peer-review filter that selected the highest-authenticity-rated LLM proposals was performed by associates within the same population that will later be on the onboarding teams. If the same people both rated the proposals for authenticity and later interview/grade them, there is a leakage problem. The cleanest mitigation is verifying that no individual is in both roles for the same proposal; this is probably already true administratively but should be confirmed.

**Panel familiarity with proposal types.** The 3-person executive panel may, over time, develop intuitions about which proposals "feel" LLM-generated. If panelists can reliably identify proposal source from the implementation plan, the grade is partially a measure of the panel's slop-detection ability rather than purely of plan quality. This is partially testable: at the end of the study, ask each panelist to retrospectively rate each proposal for likelihood of LLM origin and check whether this correlates with their grades. If a strong correlation exists, panel-source-detection becomes a covariate in the main analysis.

**Generalization beyond CMT.** The Osipov claim is not vertical-specific. Findings from CMT will need replication in other verticals (financial services, healthcare, public sector) before the diagnostic can be claimed as generally valid for the BPO/SI context. A reasonable second-wave plan is to repeat the study in one or two other verticals' onboarding exercises after the first wave reports.

**Feedback loop into the workshop arm.** If the exercise is repeated quarterly and authors of workshop-generated proposals see their proposals receive systematically higher grades than LLM-generated ones (after source revelation), the workshop arm may become contaminated by associates who deliberately produce more impressive workshop proposals to win the comparison — or, alternatively, the LLM arm may attract associates who want to "prove" the LLM approach can work and prompt-engineer harder. Either dynamic distorts the longitudinal estimate. This is the operational analog of the Osipov adaptation concern (A2 §17) and the 12-month re-measurement design from that section becomes more important, not less.

## Section 8 — Bottom-line revised plan

The protocol I would now run is materially shorter than the A2 deep-dive, materially cheaper, and stronger on the core claim being tested.

The first-wave study uses one quarterly cycle of the CMT onboarding exercise as the experimental vehicle. Required protocol additions are: instrumentation of the screening step, recording and coding of author interviews, capture of individual panelist grades, addition of a 20% randomly-assigned team subset and a 20% document-only team subset (so the operational exercise runs in three flavors: natural-screening + interview, random-assigned + interview, random-assigned + document-only), source revelation only after panel grading, and an author post-hoc survey. Cost is in the $25,000–$35,000 range, dominated by research-staff time for coding and analysis. Sample size is whatever the quarterly cohort produces (in a CMT BU running this exercise across multiple onboarding cohorts, this is plausibly 30–80 teams and 60–150 proposals, which is statistically comfortable for the primary effects).

The primary outputs are: (i) an estimate of the LLM-vs-workshop grade effect (the operational analog of Osipov's central claim, with stronger ecological validity than anything in the existing literature); (ii) a measurement of the screening-pick-rate gap (the operational analog of the Kommers superficial-competence claim); (iii) an estimate of the live-interview contribution (the operational test of the Osipov synchrony hypothesis); (iv) a measurement of LLM-augmented downstream rescue (a finding the existing literature has not addressed); (v) a measurement of source-detection accuracy by both teams and panelists (a calibration of how visible slop is to skilled humans).

This is a substantially stronger study design than the A2 deep-dive on its own, achieved primarily by recognizing that your operational process already implements the experimental conditions A2 was trying to construct synthetically. The core methodological value of the original A2 protocol — the construct definition in §1, the falsification conditions in §19, the coding scheme in §8, the statistical approach in §9 — transfers directly to this setting. The brief construction, trainee recruitment, and session-staging components of A2 are simply replaced by the existing exercise.

The single most important thing to do now, before running anything, is to verify three things about the existing exercise: (1) that the LLM-vs-workshop assignment to associates was actually random and not self-selected; (2) that the peer-review and the team-interview pools are administratively separate; (3) that the panel composition and grading rubric have been stable enough across past cycles to justify treating historical grade data as a baseline. If any of these three is not in place, the natural experiment is weakened and may need supplementary controls. If all three are in place, you have a study design that the published literature on workslop and slop generally does not yet have access to, and it is worth running with care.
